# Datathon 2026 Judging Rubric

Use this rubric to score team presentations consistently across all tracks.

## Copy/Paste Text for Judge Google Doc

Use the block below at the top of your judge packet document:

```text
Welcome, and thank you for judging Datathon 2026.

Your role is to evaluate each team fairly using evidence from their presentation, notebook, and cited data sources. Please score based on analytical quality, clarity of reasoning, and honesty about limitations. Do not score based on presentation polish alone.

Judging principles:
- Apply the same standards across all teams.
- Use the full scoring range when appropriate.
- Reward claims that are supported by data.
- Distinguish correlation from causation when evaluating conclusions.
- Leave concise comments that explain your scoring decisions.

Each team is scored out of 100 points across six categories. If required submission elements are missing, flag the issue for staff before finalizing scores.
```

## Judge Instructions

1. Score each category independently using the descriptors below.
2. Base scores on evidence shown in the presentation, notebook, and cited data sources.
3. Reward clear reasoning and honest limitations, not just polished slides.
4. Use the full score range to separate teams fairly.
5. Add brief comments for the strongest and weakest part of each presentation.

## Submission Compliance Check (Before Scoring)

Mark each item `Yes` or `No`:

1. Team selected exactly one official track.
2. Notebook file was submitted.
3. Slideshow file was submitted.
4. Last notebook cell is titled `Dataset Citations (MLA 8)`.
5. Dataset citations are present and appear complete.

If required materials are missing, notify event staff before finalizing scores.

## Scoring Scale

Use this 5-level scale in every category:

- `5 - Excellent`: Clear, rigorous, convincing, and complete.
- `4 - Strong`: Good quality with minor gaps.
- `3 - Competent`: Adequate and understandable, but limited depth.
- `2 - Needs Improvement`: Important weaknesses reduce confidence.
- `1 - Poor`: Major issues, missing evidence, or unclear work.

## Rubric Categories (100 Points Total)

| Category | Weight | What Judges Should Look For |
|---|---:|---|
| 1) Research Question and Track Fit | 15 | A specific, testable question that clearly matches the chosen track and explains why the problem matters. |
| 2) Data Quality and Preparation | 20 | Appropriate dataset choice, clear preprocessing steps, handling of missing/dirty data, and justified assumptions. |
| 3) Analysis and Evidence | 25 | Meaningful EDA/statistical analysis/modeling, correct interpretation of results, and claims supported by data visuals or metrics. |
| 4) Technical Rigor and Reproducibility | 15 | Notebook logic is coherent, methods are technically sound, workflow is reproducible, and code/results are internally consistent. |
| 5) Presentation Clarity and Storytelling | 15 | Clear structure, readable visuals, strong communication, effective pacing, and direct answers to the research question. |
| 6) Limitations, Ethics, and Practical Insight | 10 | Honest discussion of limitations, bias/risk awareness, distinction between correlation and causation, and realistic next steps. |

## Category Scoring Guide

Apply these anchors inside each category:

- `Excellent (90-100% of category weight)`: Work is complete, accurate, and insightful.
- `Strong (75-89% of category weight)`: Mostly strong with minor issues.
- `Competent (60-74% of category weight)`: Acceptable but lacks depth or precision.
- `Needs Improvement (40-59% of category weight)`: Major weaknesses in method or communication.
- `Poor (0-39% of category weight)`: Missing key elements or unsupported conclusions.

Example conversion for a 20-point category:

- Excellent: `18-20`
- Strong: `15-17`
- Competent: `12-14`
- Needs Improvement: `8-11`
- Poor: `0-7`

## Tie-Break Guidance

If two teams are tied on total score, break ties in this order:

1. Higher score in `Analysis and Evidence`
2. Higher score in `Data Quality and Preparation`
3. Higher score in `Limitations, Ethics, and Practical Insight`
4. Judge panel discussion and consensus vote
